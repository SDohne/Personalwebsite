Mini Project 2025

How does tree girth and time period influence budburst in Quercus Robur, accounting for variability across differing sampling locations?

Applied linear mixed-effects modeling in R to analyze phenological patterns in Quercus robur budburst timing across 2,405 observations from 33 locations. Implemented advanced data wrangling techniques to merge multiple datasets, stratified sampling with reproducible workflows, and hierarchical modeling with random effects to control for spatial variation. Used statistical model comparison to identify tree girth and temporal effects on budburst phenology.

About Me

I am a passionate ecological data scientist currently pursuing my MSc in Living Planet with Computational Methods in Ecology and Evolution at Imperial College London. My work sits at the intersection of ecology, data science, and computational modeling, where I apply advanced statistical and machine learning techniques to understand and predict ecological patterns.

With a strong foundation in conservation biology from the University of Exeter and professional experience as a Risk Analyst at the Animal and Plant Health Agency, I bring both theoretical knowledge and practical expertise to complex ecological challenges. My research interests span from predicting agricultural yields using satellite data to modeling disease spillover risks in wildlife populations.

I am proficient in R, Python, and various GIS tools, with particular expertise in statistical modeling, machine learning applications in ecology, and high-performance computing for large-scale ecological simulations. My goal is to leverage data-driven approaches to contribute meaningfully to conservation efforts and sustainable agricultural practices.

When I'm not analyzing data or building models, you'll find me exploring new computational techniques, contributing to open-source projects, or staying current with the latest developments in ecological modeling and remote sensing applications.
